# Prosthetic Trial Outreach Assessment

## 1. Clinical Trial Gap Analysis: Lower Limb Prosthetics

### 1.1 Current Landscape (Source: ClinicalTrials.gov Search Limitations)

> **Note:** The ClinicalTrials.gov API query interface used during this assessment had format incompatibilities with the available MCP tools. The following gap analysis is based on:
> - Known systemic patterns in prosthetic/rehabilitation medicine trials
> - Published literature on recruitment disparities in upper vs. lower limb prosthetics research
> - Demographic and geographic trends observed in FDA-device and NIH-funded prosthetic studies

### 1.2 Identified Trial Gaps

| Gap Category | Description | Underserved Population |
|---|---|---|
| **Age Bias** | Most lower-limb prosthetic trials disproportionately recruit working-age adults (25-55), excluding elderly amputees (65+) who have higher incidence of limb loss from vascular disease and falls. | Adults 65+, especially those with peripheral vascular disease-related amputations |
| **Income & Insurance** | Hi-tech microprocessor knee/ankle trials typically require participants to have insurance covering $30K-70K devices; Medicaid-only and uninsured populations are systematically excluded. | Low-income amputees, Medicaid recipients, underinsured |
| **Rural Access** | The majority of prosthetic trials are conducted at academic medical centers in large metro areas (Boston, Philly, Los Angeles, Cleveland). Rural amputees face travel-distance barriers to participation. | Rural and small-town amputees across Midwest, South, and Mountain states |
| **Ethnic & Racial Diversity** | Proportional underrepresentation of Black, Hispanic, and Indigenous amputees in prosthetic trials, partly due to distrust of research institutions and lack of culturally competent recruitment. | Black, Hispanic, Indigenous, and rural-dwelling patients |
| **Transgender & Gender-Diverse** | Almost no prosthetic trials specifically address the needs of transgender amputees (e.g., socket fitting considerations for gender-affirming hormone changes, phantom pain management differences). | Transgender and gender-diverse amputees |
| **Upper-Limb + Lower-Limb Combined** | Most trials focus on a single limb type; double-amputees and those requiring both upper- and lower-limb prosthetics are rarely enrolled. | Multiple-limb amputees, traumatic multi-limb loss survivors |
| **Pediatric & Young Adult** | Prosthetic trials rarely include minors (under 18) or young adults (18-22), who have distinct growth-plate, skeletal-maturation, and activity-level needs. | Pediatric amputees, young adults with congenital limb differences |
| **Community-Based vs. Academic** | Trials overwhelmingly use controlled lab settings (gait labs, research clinics) rather than real-world community environments, limiting ecological validity and applicability to daily life. | All amputees who use prosthetics in community settings, not just lab conditions |
| **Psychological & Phantom Pain Focus** | Very few trials address the psychological sequelae of limb loss (depression, body image, phantom limb pain) alongside prosthetic function; most focus purely on biomechanical outcomes. | Amputees with chronic phantom pain, depression, or PTSD comorbidity |
| **Low-Cost / Low-Tech Solutions** | The trend toward high-tech prosthetics in trials leaves a gap in evidence for low-cost, durable, and repairable prosthetic solutions suitable for low-resource settings and developing nations. | Low-resource settings, developing-world amputees, veterans in underserved VA facilities |

### 1.3 Sponsor & Phase Trends (General Field Knowledge)

- **Industry-sponsored trials** (Ossur, Ottobock, Ossur Canada, Blatchford) dominate Phase III device trials but tend to focus on premium-income, urban-insured populations.
- **NIH/NIDRR-funded trials** are more likely to include diverse populations but are limited by smaller sample sizes and single-site designs.
- **VA-sponsored trials** show better rural and older-adult representation but are geographically concentrated near VA medical centers.
- **Phase I/II trials** (safety/early efficacy) are heavily biased toward younger, healthier participants able to attend frequent in-person visits.
- **Phase III/IV trials** (post-market surveillance, real-world evidence) are the only ones beginning to capture broader demographic data, but participation barriers remain high.

### 1.4 Strategic Implications for Outreach

The gaps above suggest that **community-level outreach** near accessible, transit-rich sites (like Shirley Ryan AbilityLab) can specifically address:
1. **Age diversity** -- by partnering with senior centers and vascular clinics
2. **Insurance diversity** -- by offering transportation assistance and insurance-navigation support
3. **Rural reach** -- by hosting webinars and mobile prosthetic clinics
4. **Racial/ethnic diversity** -- by partnering with community health centers and faith-based organizations
5. **Psychological support** -- by co-locating mental health services at prosthetic follow-up visits

## 3. Proposed Community Outreach Strategies

### 3.1 Targeted Recruitment Programs

| Strategy | Target Gap | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| **Senior Amputee Outreach** | Age bias (65+) | Partner with senior centers in Streeterville, Gold Coast, and Near North Side; offer shuttle service from senior living facilities |
| **Medicaid & Uninsured Navigation** | Insurance/income bias | Embed insurance-navigation counselors at Shirley Ryan AbilityLab; partner with Enroll Chicago for application assistance |
| **Rural Telehealth Screenings** | Rural access barrier | Launch monthly virtual prosthetic-clinic days; partner with rural IL critical-access hospitals for in-person intake |
| **Faith- and Community-Based referrals** | Racial/ethnic diversity | Partner with churches, mosques, and community health centers on the West and South Sides for gatekeeper training |
| **Veterans Affairs coordination** | Multiple gaps | Formalize referral pipeline with VA Illinois Health System; VA patients often fall into multiple underserved categories |

### 3.2 Site-Level Accessibility Improvements

| Improvement | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Pre-arranged parking validation** | Negotiate with 2-3 nearby Streeterville garages for prosthetic-appointment parking discounts |
| **On-site or mobile pharmacy** | Partner with a nearby pharmacy for home-delivery or scheduled pick-up for prosthetic-related wound care and pain management |
| **Transit subsidy program** | Provide CTA Ventra card loadings or rideshare credits for patients attending intensive prosthetic-rehab sessions (3+ weeks) |
| **Weekend and evening clinic hours** | Accommodate patients who work hourly-shift jobs and cannot attend weekday daytime appointments |
| **Childcare during appointments** | Offer on-site childcare to enable caregivers (often elderly spouses) to accompany young amputee patients |

### 3.3 Data and Measurement Framework

| Metric | Target | Measurement Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Diversity index** (age, race, insurance) of recruited prosthetic trial participants | Match Chicago demographic proportions within 10% | Baseline audit of current trial rosters; quarterly review |
| **Rural participant travel time** | Reduce median travel time to < 90 min | GPS tracking or self-reported travel logs |
| **No-show rate by demographic** | Reduce disparities to < 5 percentage points | Compare no-show rates across age, race, insurance groups |
| **Community referral conversion** | 20% of community-referred patients enroll in a trial | Track referral source in intake forms |
| **Participant satisfaction** | >= 4.0 / 5.0 on accessibility survey | Post-appointment survey (paper + digital) |

### 3.4 Phased Rollout

| Phase | Timeline | Key Actions |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1: Foundation** | Months 1-3 | Map current prosthetic trial demographics; establish community health center partnerships; launch transit subsidy pilot |
| **Phase 2: Expansion** | Months 4-9 | Roll out senior outreach and faith-based referral programs; begin telehealth rural screenings |
| **Phase 3: Integration** | Months 10-12 | Integrate insurance-navigation services on-site; publish diversity dashboard; scale successful pilots to other Chicago rehabilitation sites |

## 4. Summary and Recommendations

The Shirley Ryan AbilityLab is excellently positioned as a community outreach hub for prosthetic-trial recruitment because of:
- **Strong transit coverage** (multiple CTA bus stops within 500 m)
- **Anchor-institution credibility** (rehabilitation research hospital)
- **Dense amenity ecosystem** (food, banking, hotel for out-of-town patients)

**Key areas where intervention is most needed:**
1. **Parking and transit subsidies** -- the single highest barrier for low-income and elderly prosthetic users
2. **Pharmacy access** -- no on-site pharmacy; mobile or delivery pharmacy partnerships are critical
3. **Racial and age diversity** -- community-based referral pipelines through faith organizations and senior centers
4. **Rural reach** -- telehealth bridge to connect rural amputees with trial opportunities

This report should be treated as a living document and updated as ClinicalTrials.gov search capacity improves and as enrollment data from active prosthetic trials becomes available for quantitative analysis.
